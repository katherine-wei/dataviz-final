The Story

In a few sentences:

After 150 years as a British colony, Hong Kong was torn down the middle when it approached the handover back to China in 1997. Many families fled under the pretense of a graceful visit overseas, knowing that the Chinese government’s communist agenda did not bode well for the future, while the remaining majority were excited for their “original” government to reclaim the city. Aside from the celebrations and pompous ceremonies, the people did not immediately feel the impact of the handover; the British had negotiated with China to extend a 50-year protection of civil liberties, open markets that would give way to a “gradual democratization." China's then-leader, Deng Xiaoping, agreed.

"The racehorses may gallop and the people dance, but only for another 50 years."

Deng Xiaoping, 1997

Their Identities

Polls have steadily shown that larger and larger percentages of Hong Kong citizens identify as “Hong Konger” rather than “Chinese” even as the government seeks greater conformity. To many in Hong Kong, then, “Chinese” may primarily mean a cultural, ethnic, or racial marker of identity rather than of political nationality. There are “Chinese” of various types who make up the majority population in Taiwan and Singapore, a significant percentage in Malaysia and Thailand, and large numbers around the world.
